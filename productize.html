Positioning

Outcome: one sentence that says who this is for and the one result it sells. Specific owner, specific outcome.

You built the whole thing for yourself. That is the unfair advantage. You are not selling AI consulting, you are selling the exact result you already have proof of. So you do not start with the product. You start with the person and the outcome.

"AI consulting" is dead on arrival. Nobody wants AI. They want their inbox handled, their content shipped, their leads followed up. Name the owner who has the pain you already solved, and name the one thing they get.

Prompt: Here is my soul file, my department briefs, and my archive. Based on what I actually built and the results I have, who is the one type of owner this helps most, and what is the single outcome they would pay for. Give me three positioning sentences in the format: I help [who] [get one outcome] without [the pain they expect].

Pick one. A real estate broker who wants 30 days of content without writing it. A founder who wants every lead followed up the same day without a hire. Specific beats clever. The narrower the owner, the easier the yes.

Do this

  1. Feed the system your own briefs and results, and run the positioning prompt.
  2. Choose the one owner and the one outcome you can prove.
  3. Write it as a single sentence you can say out loud without flinching.

The offer

Outcome: a named, scoped, priced offer with the value equation behind the number.

Positioning is who and what. The offer is how it is packaged and what it costs. Name it so it sounds like a thing, not a meeting. Scope it so both of you know where it ends. Price it on the value of the outcome, not the hours you put in.

The four pieces

  • Name. Something that says the result. "The Content Engine install," not "AI services."
  • Scope. The exact departments you stand up, what they hand over, and what they do not. Draw the line.
  • Price. Anchored to the outcome. If it replaces a hire or a five-figure agency, price near that, not near your cost.
  • Proof. The receipts from your own build. You ran it on yourself first. Show that.

Run it through the value equation before you set the number. More dream outcome and more likelihood it works push the price up. Less time to result and less effort asked of them push it up too. Your system is strong on all four, because it already runs without you. Charge like it.

Do this

  1. Name the offer after the result, not the toolset.
  2. Write the scope as a short list of departments installed and what each delivers.
  3. Set the price against the outcome, then pressure-test it with the value equation.

Build and sell

Outcome: the system markets and sells the very thing it is part of. The product is its own best demo.

Here is the loop that closes the course. You stood up a content department and an outreach department to run your business. Now you point them at selling the offer. The thing that sells the system is the system.

  • Content sells it. The content engine ships build-in-public teardowns of what you actually shipped. Every post is a receipt. The proof is the product running in public.
  • Outreach sells it. The outreach department books calls with the exact owner from your positioning. Instantly for cold email, your CRM for follow-up, all of it already built.
  • Sales closes it. Fathom records the calls, the system drafts the recap and the proposal, and you do the handshake.

This is the proof a prospect cannot argue with. You are not telling them a content engine works. They found you through yours. The funnel that sold them is the funnel they are buying.

Do this

  1. Point the content department at the offer: teardowns of your real build, one receipt per post.
  2. Aim the outreach department at the owner from Lesson 12.1.
  3. Let the sales flow handle recap and proposal, and book the close.

Install for clients

Outcome: the whole stack packaged as a repeatable install, the same blocks in the same order, so onboarding is a runbook.

You built this once, for you, and it was a discovery. Doing it for a client should not be a discovery. It should be a runbook. Same foundation, same memory, same hands, same departments, in the same order, every time. The only thing that changes is whose business goes inside.

The receipt is right in front of you. This curriculum is that productized system. Foundation, second brain, the hands, the eight departments, the team, the harden pass, in order. That is the install. You are reading the runbook.

  • The same order. Build the brain, give it hands, stand up departments, name the team, lock it down. You never reinvent the sequence.
  • Client soul file first. Run the interview on them, not you. Their identity, their voice, their buckets. The blocks are fixed, the contents are theirs.
  • One module per session. Onboarding becomes a schedule, not a scramble. Each block has a done-when, so you always know where you are.

Do this

  1. Turn the module order into a numbered install checklist, one block per step.
  2. Swap your soul-file interview for a client version, so the system learns them instead of you.
  3. Run the first install against the runbook and time it. The second one is faster.
